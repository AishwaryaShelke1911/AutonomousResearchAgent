## Introduction

The global energy landscape is undergoing its most dramatic transformation since the Industrial Revolution. Renewable energy — encompassing solar, wind, hydroelectric, geothermal, and emerging technologies like green hydrogen — has moved from a niche, subsidy-dependent sector to the dominant force in new electricity generation worldwide. In 2024, renewables accounted for over 90% of all new electricity capacity added globally, a milestone that would have seemed implausible two decades ago. This transition is being driven by a powerful confluence of forces: rapidly falling technology costs, urgent climate imperatives, energy security concerns amplified by geopolitical instability, and growing investment from both public and private sectors.

The cost revolution in renewables has been extraordinary. The price of solar photovoltaic (PV) electricity has fallen by over 90% in the last decade, making solar the cheapest source of electricity in history in most of the world. Onshore wind costs have dropped by 70% over the same period. These cost declines have made renewable energy economically competitive — and increasingly superior — to fossil fuels in nearly every market, without accounting for the significant external costs of carbon emissions and air pollution associated with coal, oil, and natural gas.

The year 2025 stands as a pivotal moment in this transition. Global renewable energy capacity crossed the 5 terawatt (TW) milestone — enough to power roughly half of current global electricity demand. Countries from Germany to Vietnam to Chile are setting ambitious 100% renewable electricity targets. The International Energy Agency (IEA) forecasts that renewables will surpass coal as the largest source of global electricity generation by 2025-2026, a tipping point with profound implications for climate, geopolitics, and the global economy.

This report synthesizes the most significant trends in renewable energy in 2025, examining key technological and policy developments, identifying challenges to the energy transition, and exploring the future scope of a fully renewable global energy system.

## Key Findings

**1. Solar Power: The World's Cheapest Energy Source**
Solar PV has become the fastest-growing energy technology in history. In 2024, the world installed a record 450 gigawatts (GW) of solar capacity — more than the entire installed base of solar energy just five years earlier. The average global cost of utility-scale solar electricity fell to $0.033/kWh in 2024, compared to $0.30/kWh for coal plants and $0.25/kWh for natural gas. China remains the world's largest solar market, adding 230 GW in 2024 alone, followed by the United States (40 GW) and India (35 GW). New perovskite-silicon tandem solar cells achieved 33.9% efficiency in laboratory settings — approaching the theoretical maximum — signaling a coming generation of even more powerful panels. Floating solar installations on reservoirs and offshore platforms are opening new land-constrained markets.

**2. Wind Power Expansion On- and Offshore**
Wind energy continues its rapid global expansion, with total installed capacity exceeding 1,100 GW by end of 2024. The offshore wind sector is experiencing particularly dynamic growth — costs have fallen 60% since 2015 and offshore wind is now competitive with natural gas in many European and Asian markets. The UK, Germany, and the Netherlands are racing toward their combined target of 300 GW of offshore capacity by 2050. The United States offshore wind sector, despite near-term project delays due to supply chain pressures and interest rate headwinds in 2023-2024, is recovering with strong federal support from the Inflation Reduction Act. Next-generation floating offshore wind turbines — capable of operating in waters too deep for fixed-bottom foundations — are entering commercial deployment, dramatically expanding the geography of viable offshore wind resources.

**3. Green Hydrogen: The Energy Carrier of the Future**
Green hydrogen — produced by using renewable electricity to split water into hydrogen and oxygen via electrolysis — has emerged as a crucial solution for decarbonizing sectors that cannot be directly electrified, including heavy industry (steel, cement, chemicals), long-haul shipping, and aviation. The global green hydrogen market attracted over $570 billion in announced investments by 2025. Electrolyzer manufacturing costs have fallen 50% since 2020, and analysts project green hydrogen will reach cost parity with fossil-fuel-derived "grey" hydrogen in most markets by 2027-2030. The EU's REPowerEU plan targets 10 million tonnes of domestic green hydrogen production per year by 2030. Major projects in Australia, Chile, Saudi Arabia, and Morocco are developing gigawatt-scale green hydrogen facilities for both domestic use and export.

**4. Battery Storage Reaching Grid Scale**
Energy storage is solving the longstanding "intermittency challenge" for solar and wind — the problem that the sun doesn't always shine and the wind doesn't always blow. Grid-scale lithium-ion battery storage capacity more than tripled between 2022 and 2025, reaching 350 GWh globally. The US and China are leading deployments, with the US adding over 30 GW of battery storage in 2024 under Inflation Reduction Act incentives. Emerging long-duration energy storage technologies — including iron-air batteries (Form Energy), flow batteries, compressed air energy storage, and green hydrogen storage — are targeting durations of 10-100+ hours, enabling renewable energy to power grids through multi-day weather events. Falling battery costs (now below $100/kWh for utility-scale systems) are also supercharging the electric vehicle revolution, which is creating billions of dollars in additional battery manufacturing scale.

**5. Policy Momentum: IRA, REPowerEU, and National Net-Zero Commitments**
Policy has been a crucial accelerant for renewable energy. The U.S. Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), passed in 2022, provides an estimated $369 billion in clean energy incentives over 10 years and is projected to catalyze over $3 trillion in private clean energy investment by 2032, transforming the U.S. into a clean energy manufacturing powerhouse. The EU's REPowerEU plan targets 45% renewable electricity by 2030, up from its prior 40% target, in response to energy security imperatives following Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Over 130 countries have now committed to net-zero emissions targets, and the global tally of jurisdictions with renewable energy targets has risen to 170. At the 2023 COP28 climate summit, nations agreed to triple renewable energy capacity by 2030 — from approximately 3.4 TW to over 11 TW — a commitment that, if fulfilled, would fundamentally reshape global energy systems.

**6. Emerging Markets Leapfrogging to Renewables**
Just as developing nations bypassed landline telephone infrastructure and leapfrogged directly to mobile phones, many emerging economies are now skipping fossil fuel buildout and deploying renewables as their primary electricity source. India added a record 32 GW of renewable capacity in 2024 and is on track for its target of 500 GW by 2030. Vietnam rapidly expanded its solar capacity from near zero to over 20 GW in just five years. Countries in sub-Saharan Africa, where over 600 million people lack electricity access, are deploying decentralized solar mini-grids and rooftop solar at scale, providing reliable electricity without requiring expensive transmission infrastructure. The rapidly falling cost of solar PV and battery storage has made off-grid renewable systems the most cost-effective path to electricity access for remote communities globally.

**7. Corporate Energy Procurement Driving Demand**
The private sector has become a major driver of renewable energy deployment through corporate Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) and self-generation. In 2024, corporations globally signed over 50 GW of PPAs — long-term contracts to purchase renewable electricity directly from generators — a new record. Technology companies (Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta), manufacturing conglomerates, and financial institutions are racing to meet 100% renewable electricity commitments, driven by investor pressure, regulatory requirements (particularly in Europe), and cost savings as renewables undercut grid electricity prices. This corporate demand is proving essential for financing new projects in markets where government incentives are limited, and is creating new financing models that are accelerating deployment.

## Challenges

**1. Grid Infrastructure and Transmission Bottlenecks**
The rapid scaling of solar and wind generation is exposing critical weaknesses in electricity grid infrastructure built for centralized fossil fuel generation. Renewable energy resources are often located far from population centers — solar in deserts, wind in rural plains and coasts — requiring massive new transmission lines to deliver power to consumers. In the U.S., permitting and building new transmission lines takes 10-15 years on average due to complex multi-jurisdictional approvals, community opposition, and coordination challenges. Over 2,000 GW of renewable energy projects are reportedly waiting in grid interconnection queues in the U.S. alone. Upgrading grids to handle bidirectional power flows from distributed solar and batteries, and to integrate large shares of variable renewable generation, will require hundreds of billions in global infrastructure investment.

**2. Critical Mineral Supply Chains and Resource Constraints**
The clean energy transition is heavily dependent on specific critical minerals: lithium, cobalt, nickel, and manganese for batteries; silver, indium, and tellurium for solar panels; neodymium and dysprosium for wind turbine magnets; and platinum group metals for electrolyzers. Global demand for these minerals is projected to increase 4-6x by 2040. Mining these materials raises concerns about environmental impacts, community rights violations, and supply chain concentration — the Democratic Republic of Congo produces 70% of global cobalt, while China refines 60-80% of most critical minerals. Geopolitical competition over mineral resources is intensifying, with the U.S., EU, Australia, and Canada pursuing "friend-shoring" strategies to reduce dependence on China. Circular economy approaches — battery recycling, mineral recovery — will be essential to manage supply constraints long-term.

**3. Balancing Variable Generation and Grid Stability**
As solar and wind provide an ever-larger share of electricity, managing grid stability becomes increasingly challenging. Solar and wind generate electricity when the sun shines and wind blows — not necessarily when demand peaks. In some markets (California, Germany, Spain), renewables already sometimes generate more electricity than the grid can absorb, leading to curtailment (wasting clean energy) and even negative electricity prices. Conversely, periods of low renewable output require "backup" generation or storage. Managing these dynamics requires sophisticated grid operations, new market mechanisms to value flexibility, expanded interconnections between grids, demand response programs that shift electricity consumption to periods of high renewable generation, and large-scale energy storage deployment. As renewable shares approach and exceed 80-90%, maintaining grid stability requires fundamentally new approaches.

**4. Financing Gaps in Developing Nations**
Despite falling technology costs, clean energy investment in low-and-middle-income countries remains dramatically below what's needed. The IEA estimates that developing nations (excluding China) need to more than quadruple annual clean energy investment from roughly $300 billion to over $1 trillion by 2030 to stay on track for net-zero goals. Structural barriers — high perceived risk, limited domestic capital markets, weak credit ratings, currency volatility — drive up financing costs, making renewable projects in developing countries significantly more expensive than comparable projects in OECD nations. Mechanisms to mobilize concessional international financing (multilateral development banks, climate funds, blended finance) are scaling but not fast enough. Without dramatically increasing clean energy access in emerging markets, global climate targets cannot be achieved.

**5. Social Acceptance and "Energy Justice" Concerns**
Renewable energy development is not without local controversy. Large-scale wind and solar projects can face opposition from local communities concerned about visual impacts on landscapes, noise from wind turbines, land use conflicts with agriculture, and impacts on local wildlife (bird and bat mortality from wind turbines; habitat disruption from utility-scale solar). Indigenous community rights and Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) must be respected for projects on or near traditional lands. Just transition concerns are acute in communities dependent on coal mining and fossil fuel industries, where rapid decarbonization threatens jobs and local tax bases. Energy justice advocates emphasize that the benefits of clean energy — lower costs, reduced air pollution — must be equitably shared, with historically marginalized communities not bearing disproportionate burdens from both fossil fuels and renewable development.

**6. Technology Gaps for Hard-to-Decarbonize Sectors**
Electrification with renewables is straightforward for electricity generation and many transportation and heating applications, but certain sectors face deep technological challenges. Aviation currently has no commercially viable zero-carbon solution — sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) from biomass or green hydrogen is at early stages and far more expensive than jet fuel. Maritime shipping (responsible for 3% of global emissions) faces similar challenges with green methanol and ammonia as potential fuels still at demonstration scale. Cement production (8% of global CO₂) involves chemical reactions that produce CO₂ regardless of energy source, requiring carbon capture or alternative chemistry. Steel production is transitioning toward green hydrogen-based direct reduction, but at slow pace. These hard-to-abate sectors will require sustained R&D investment, supportive policy, and time to transition.

## Future Scope

**1. Solar and Wind Achieving Near-Total Grid Penetration**
The IEA's Net Zero Emissions by 2050 scenario envisions solar and wind providing nearly 70% of global electricity by 2050, up from roughly 13% in 2023. This trajectory implies installing roughly 630 GW of new solar and 390 GW of new wind annually through 2030 — nearly double current installation rates. Next-generation solar technologies — perovskite tandem cells, bifacial panels, building-integrated photovoltaics — will push efficiencies above 40% and open new installation contexts. Agrivoltaics (co-locating solar panels with agricultural crops) can simultaneously generate clean electricity and improve crop yields by providing shade and reducing water evaporation, resolving land use conflicts. Advanced AI-driven grid management systems will optimize renewable dispatch, demand response, and storage dispatch in real time across increasingly complex grid architectures.

**2. Green Hydrogen Economy Scaling to Global Markets**
By 2030-2035, analysts project green hydrogen production costs will fall to $1-2/kg in high-solar and high-wind resource regions, making it cost-competitive with natural gas for industrial applications. This will unlock a global green hydrogen trade — Australia, Chile, Morocco, and the Middle East are positioning as major exporters. Green hydrogen will be essential for decarbonizing steel (via direct reduction), ammonia production (for fertilizers and shipping fuel), chemical feedstocks, and long-haul transport. Hydrogen pipelines and refueling infrastructure are being planned and built across Europe, Japan, South Korea, and China. The green hydrogen economy could exceed $11 trillion in cumulative value by 2050, according to BloombergNEF, representing one of the largest market creation opportunities in energy history.

**3. Fusion Energy: From Science Experiment to Potential Commercial Reality**
After decades of being perpetually "30 years away," nuclear fusion — the process that powers the sun — is showing unprecedented commercial momentum. In December 2022, the National Ignition Facility achieved the world's first fusion ignition, producing more energy from fusion reactions than the laser energy used to trigger them. Private fusion companies including Commonwealth Fusion Systems, TAE Technologies, Helion Energy (backed by Microsoft with a power purchase agreement), and over 40 others are collectively attracting over $6 billion in private investment. Several companies target demonstration plants by 2030-2035 and commercial power plants by 2040. While significant scientific and engineering challenges remain, fusion energy's potential — virtually limitless fuel from seawater, no long-lived radioactive waste, no meltdown risk — makes it the most transformative potential energy technology on the horizon.

**4. Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) and Distributed Energy Resources**
The electrification of transportation creates a massive, distributed battery network that can interact dynamically with the grid. Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) technology allows electric vehicles to discharge stored energy back to the grid during peak demand, effectively turning millions of EVs into distributed storage resources. The IEA estimates that EV batteries globally will have a combined storage capacity of 14 TWh by 2030 — more than 10x current grid-scale battery storage capacity. When managed with smart charging infrastructure and AI optimization, this fleet can provide enormous grid flexibility services. V2G pilots are advancing rapidly in Europe, Japan, and California, with commercial programs expected to become mainstream by 2028-2030. Combined with smart home energy management systems and distributed rooftop solar, V2G represents the vision of a fully distributed, democratized energy system.

**5. AI-Optimized Renewable Energy Systems**
Artificial intelligence will become deeply integrated into every aspect of the renewable energy value chain. AI weather forecasting models are already dramatically improving the accuracy of solar and wind generation predictions, enabling better grid planning. Machine learning algorithms optimize wind turbine blade pitch and yaw in real time, improving energy capture by 3-5%. AI-driven predictive maintenance detects equipment anomalies before failures occur, reducing operations and maintenance costs by 20-30%. At the grid level, AI coordinates millions of distributed assets — solar panels, batteries, EVs, smart appliances — in real time to balance supply and demand. DeepMind's AI reduced Google's data center cooling energy use by 40%; similar approaches applied at grid scale represent enormous efficiency opportunities. AI will also accelerate renewable energy material discovery, optimizing new solar cell compositions and battery chemistries with orders of magnitude faster iteration than traditional laboratory methods.

**6. Energy Storage Breakthroughs and Long-Duration Solutions**
The final piece of the 100% renewable energy puzzle is cost-effective long-duration energy storage — systems that can store energy for days, weeks, or even seasons. Multiple technology pathways are advancing: iron-air batteries (Form Energy) claim costs of $20/kWh for 100-hour storage; gravitational storage systems (Energy Vault) use renewable electricity to lift and lower heavy masses; pumped hydro remains the most mature long-duration storage technology; and underground compressed air storage is advancing at scale. Green ammonia and green hydrogen stored in existing natural gas infrastructure offer seasonal storage potential. By 2035, analysts expect long-duration storage costs to fall sufficiently to enable reliable 100% renewable electricity systems without backup fossil fuels — the final milestone in the energy transition.

## Conclusion

Renewable energy is no longer the energy of the future — it is unambiguously the energy of the present. The cost revolution in solar and wind, combined with accelerating battery storage deployment, has made the economic case for fossil fuels increasingly untenable in the electricity sector. The question is no longer *whether* the energy transition will happen, but *how fast* it will proceed — and who will benefit from it. The technical and economic barriers to a renewable-dominated energy system have largely been overcome; the remaining challenges are primarily political, financial, and logistical.

The stakes could not be higher. The energy transition is the central front of humanity's response to climate change, with the decisions made in the next 5-10 years largely determining whether the world can limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels. But the transition is also an enormous economic opportunity — creating millions of jobs in manufacturing, construction, and operations; reducing energy costs for households and businesses; improving air quality; and enhancing energy security. Nations and companies that lead the clean energy transition will gain strategic competitive advantages that compound over decades.

The path forward requires sustained action across multiple dimensions: continued technology innovation, enabling policy frameworks, dramatically scaled international financing for developing nations, upgraded grid infrastructure, and an inclusive approach that ensures energy justice and a fair transition for workers and communities dependent on fossil fuels. Renewable energy is proving itself not just as a climate solution, but as the foundation of a more prosperous, secure, and equitable global energy system. The transition is accelerating — the task now is to ensure it moves fast enough to matter.
